Alerts and watchlists

By Honestly · Track the market without starting the search again

Property markets move in bursts. A price drops. A new listing appears. A mortgage rate shifts. If you are not watching when it happens, you miss it — and by the time you check again, the opportunity is gone.

This page covers what to track, why to track it, and how to set up monitoring so you are notified when something changes — not when you remember to look.

What to monitor

Price drops

A saved property reduces its asking price. The single most actionable alert for buyers — signals a motivated seller.

New listings

A property matching your criteria comes onto the market. Be the first viewer, not the tenth.

Sold prices

A comparable property nearby completes. The Land Registry record updates — your valuation anchor shifts.

Mortgage rate changes

The Bank of England changes the base rate, or a lender reprices. Affects affordability, monthly payments, and buyer demand.

Days on market

A watched property crosses 30, 60, or 90 days unsold. Each threshold changes the negotiation dynamics.

Under offer / back to market

A property goes under offer (urgency) or returns to market after a fall-through (opportunity at a likely discount).

Price-drop alerts — the buyer's best tool

A price reduction is the clearest signal a seller is motivated. It means the initial asking price was not supported by viewings and offers, and the seller has accepted reality. The reduction itself is useful — but the context around it is more useful.

First reduction vs subsequent reductions: The first price drop often brings the property into line with the sold comparables. A second or third drop signals something deeper — the seller is under time pressure, the property has a defect the market has identified, or the agent is managing a client who will not accept the true market value. Each successive reduction weakens the seller's negotiating position.

Size of reduction: A 2% reduction on a £500,000 property is £10,000 — barely a rounding error in most markets. It signals that the seller (or agent) is testing the market rather than accepting the evidence. A 5-10% reduction is a genuine reset. Anything above 10% suggests distress or a structural issue with the valuation.

Time since reduction: A price drop that happened yesterday is a signal. A price drop that happened three months ago and the property still has not sold is evidence that the new price is also wrong.

New-listing alerts — speed matters

In a competitive market, the best properties go under offer within days — sometimes within hours. If you are relying on weekly Rightmove alerts or a weekend browse, you are already too late for the most desirable listings.

A new-listing alert should notify you within minutes of a property appearing that matches your criteria: postcode area, property type, bedrooms, and price band. The notification should include the asking price, the floor area if available, and a direct link to the listing. You should be able to dismiss it or save it with one tap.

Do not set alerts for criteria you are not genuinely ready to act on. "Just browsing" alerts create notification fatigue. When a real opportunity appears, you will ignore it because you have trained yourself to ignore all of them.

Sold-price alerts — the evidence updates

The Land Registry publishes Price Paid Data monthly. Each release adds new transactions to the record. If you are monitoring a specific area — because you are about to buy, sell, or refinance — each new sold price is a data point that shifts the valuation anchor.

A sold-price alert should tell you:

  • The address (or at minimum the street and property type)
  • The sold price and date
  • How it compares to the current median for that postcode district
  • Whether it strengthens or weakens the case for your target price

A single new comparable can change a valuation by 2-5%. Five new comparables in one direction can shift it by 10% or more. The market does not wait for you to catch up.

Mortgage-rate alerts

The Bank of England Monetary Policy Committee meets eight times a year. Each meeting can change the base rate. Each change flows through to variable-rate mortgages within weeks and to fixed-rate pricing within days.

A mortgage-rate alert should notify you of:

  • Bank of England base rate changes (immediate impact on trackers, signal for fixes)
  • Major lender repricing (when 2-3 of the big six lenders move their fixed rates by 10bp or more)
  • Swap rate moves (the wholesale cost of fixed-rate funding — leads retail rates by 2-4 weeks)

A 0.25% rate change on a £300,000 mortgage over 25 years is approximately £40/month. That is £12,000 over the life of a typical fixed term. It is worth knowing about.

Watchlists — save and monitor

A watchlist is a persistent list of properties or areas you are tracking. It is the difference between actively searching and passively monitoring. You add properties when you find them. The system alerts you when they change.

A useful watchlist shows:

  • The current status of each property (available, under offer, sold, withdrawn)
  • The price history — original asking, each reduction, and the current asking
  • Days on market for each listing
  • Whether any new comparables have appeared nearby since you added it

The watchlist should work passively. You should not need to log in and check. When something changes, you should be notified. When nothing changes, you should hear nothing.

When to use alerts vs when to search

Alerts are for monitoring a decision you have already made — you know the area, the budget, and the property type. Searching is for forming the decision — exploring areas, calibrating budgets, understanding what you can get. Use alerts when you are ready to act. Use search when you are still learning. Mixing the two creates noise in both directions.

What not to do

Do not set alerts on every property you view. Your watchlist should be short — 5-10 properties maximum. If you are watching 50 properties, you are not watching any of them. You are collecting.

Do not ignore alerts for months. If a property has been on your watchlist for six months and you have not viewed it, remove it. You are not going to buy it. The alert is noise.

Do not rely on portal email alerts. Rightmove and Zoopla instant alerts are fine for new listings, but they do not cover price drops comprehensively, they do not include sold prices at all, and they cannot cross-reference mortgage rates or market conditions. Use them as one input, not the only input.
