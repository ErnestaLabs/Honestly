Headlines vs the record

By Honestly · Checking claims against HM Land Registry data

Every week, property portals, estate agents and news outlets publish market commentary. Some of it is accurate. Some of it is noise. Almost none of it links to the underlying data so you can check it yourself.

This page shows you how to read the claims against the record — the HM Land Registry Price Paid Data, the UK House Price Index, and the live listing data — so you know which numbers to trust and which to ignore.

The three sources every property headline comes from

Before you can evaluate a claim, you need to know which dataset it is built on. There are only three.

1. HM Land Registry Price Paid Data

This is the definitive record. Every residential property sale in England and Wales — the actual price, the actual date, the actual address. Nothing else comes close. It is published monthly under the Open Government Licence. It is free. It is the floor.

What it tells you: What homes actually sold for. Not what someone asked. Not what a model guessed. What changed hands.

Its limitation: It lags. Sales are registered weeks or months after completion. The most recent month always has incomplete data. Anyone quoting "latest" Price Paid figures without noting the lag is misleading you.

2. The UK House Price Index (UK HPI)

Published by the ONS and HM Land Registry. A hedonic index that adjusts for property type, size and location mix. It is the official measure of house price inflation. It is a national and regional index — not a property-level valuation.

What it tells you: Whether prices are rising or falling, and by roughly how much, at a regional or national level.

Its limitation: It cannot tell you what a specific home is worth. Mix-adjustment smooths out the extremes. If you see a headline that says "Prices up 3% in London," that is the HPI — and it applies to the average home, not your home.

3. Live listing data (asking prices)

Rightmove, Zoopla and OnTheMarket publish asking-price indices. These measure what sellers hope to get — not what buyers actually pay. The gap between asking and sold is the single most important number in any market claim, and it is almost never shown.

What it tells you: Seller confidence. How the market feels from the vendor side.

Its limitation: It is not a sold price. In a falling market, asking prices lag sold prices. In a rising market, they lead. Neither direction is "wrong," but blending the two without labelling the source is a category error.

How to read a Zoopla market report

Zoopla publishes a monthly House Price Index. It is widely quoted because Zoopla has the largest listing database in the UK. Here is what you need to know before you cite it.

What Zoopla measures

Zoopla's index is built from mortgage valuations and agreed sale prices — not completed sales. This means it catches price changes earlier than the Land Registry (by about 3-6 months), but it also means the sample is restricted to homes that went through a mortgage valuation. Cash buyers, auction sales and new builds are underrepresented.

The Zoopla headline number — "UK house prices up 1.5% year on year" — is a mix-adjusted index, similar in method to the ONS HPI. It is reasonable as a trend indicator. But it is not a sold price. If you are buying or selling a specific property, the Zoopla index tells you nothing about what similar homes nearby actually sold for.

The Zoopla local estimate — the number that appears on every listing page — is a different animal entirely. It is an Automated Valuation Model (AVM). Zoopla does not disclose its methodology in detail. You cannot see which comparables it used, what adjustments it made, or how confident it is. It is a black box with a pound sign.

Zoopla's market commentary is well-written and widely cited because their press office is excellent. But every claim in a Zoopla report traces to one of two sources: their own AVM (which you cannot verify) or the Land Registry (which you can). If the claim is about "agreed sales" or "buyer demand," it is Zoopla's data. If it is about "sold prices," it should be Land Registry — and you can check it yourself.

How to read a Rightmove House Price Index

Rightmove's index is the most widely reported property metric in the UK press. It is also the least useful for anyone making a financial decision.

What Rightmove actually measures

Rightmove's index is purely asking prices — the initial listing price of properties coming onto the market. It is not a sold price, not a valuation, and not an agreed sale. It is what sellers hope to get on day one of marketing. No transaction has occurred.

The Rightmove index is useful for exactly one thing: measuring seller sentiment. When it rises, vendors are confident. When it falls, they are nervous. But it has zero correlation with what homes actually sell for. In some markets, the gap between Rightmove's asking-price index and the Land Registry's sold-price data is 5-10% or more.

Rightmove publishes asking-price data by region and property type. The regional breakdowns have very small sample sizes in some months. A 2% monthly move in "London flats" might represent 50 listings in a quiet month. Treat all sub-regional Rightmove moves with extreme caution.

The monthly Rightmove press release is a masterclass in narrative framing. The same 0.3% monthly rise can be "market resilient despite headwinds" in one release and "monthly growth slows as affordability bites" in the next. Read the number, ignore the adjective.

Estate agent market commentary — what to ignore

Every estate agency chain publishes a monthly market report. They are not lying. But they have an incentive you need to account for.

The structural bias: An estate agent's market report is written by their in-house research team, which reports to the marketing director, whose job is to generate instructions. The report must be factually accurate, but it will never say "now is a terrible time to sell." It will say "the market is normalising" or "conditions are balanced" or "opportunities remain for correctly priced properties."

What to actually read: The raw numbers. If an agent's report says "instructed stock is up 12% year on year," that is useful. If it says "the market continues to show remarkable resilience," that is padding. Strip the adjectives. Keep the integers.

The local vs national problem: Chain agents publish national reports built from their branch-level data. The sample in any given postcode district might be 3-4 properties. A "local market report" that aggregates three branches across a city tells you almost nothing about the street you are buying on.

The four questions to ask of any market headline

Every property market claim — in a newspaper, on a portal, in an agent's window — can be stress-tested with four questions. If the answer to any of them is "no" or "we don't know," the claim is weaker than it looks.

1. Is this a sold price or an asking price? If the source is Rightmove, it is asking. If it is the Land Registry, it is sold. If it is a lender's index (Halifax, Nationwide), it is mortgage-approved prices from that lender's book. Each measures something different. None is the full picture.

2. What is the sample size? A 5% monthly move based on 12 transactions in a postcode district is noise. A 0.5% move based on 2,000 transactions is signal. Any claim that does not disclose the underlying transaction count is hiding the most important number.

3. Is it mix-adjusted? If more four-bedroom houses sold this month than last month, the average price will rise even if every individual home is worth the same. Mix-adjustment controls for this. The Land Registry HPI is mix-adjusted. The raw median sold price is not. A claim that says "average price up 8%" without specifying whether it is mix-adjusted or raw is impossible to evaluate.

4. Can I verify it myself? HM Land Registry Price Paid Data is free and searchable. If a claim is based on sold prices, you should be able to find the underlying transactions. If you cannot, the claim is not falsifiable — which means it is not evidence.
