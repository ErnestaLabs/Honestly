Compare homes side by side

By Honestly · The numbers that actually matter when comparing properties

Most people compare homes the wrong way. They look at photos, count bedrooms, and compare asking prices. None of those tells you what a home is worth, whether it is fairly priced, or which of two similar-looking properties is the better deal.

Here is how to compare homes using the numbers that actually move the decision — sold prices, price per square metre, days on market, and rental yield.

The five numbers you need to compare any two homes

Sold price

£X
What nearby homes actually sold for — not what someone is asking. The Land Registry record, verifiable by anyone.

£ per sqm

£X/m²
Price divided by floor area. The only way to compare a 2-bed flat against a 3-bed house fairly. Strips out size bias.

Days on market

X days
How long similar homes sit before selling. Below 30 days = hot market. Above 90 = something is wrong with price or property.

Asking vs sold gap

X%
The discount from initial asking price to final sold price. A wide gap signals overpricing. A narrow gap signals accurate pricing.

Gross yield

X%
Annual rent divided by purchase price. Below 4% = capital appreciation play. Above 6% = cash flow play.

Why asking prices are useless for comparison

Two homes on the same street, both listed at £500,000. One sells for £485,000 after 3 weeks. The other sits for 6 months, drops to £450,000, and sells for £430,000. If you only looked at asking prices, you would think they were worth the same. The sold record tells you they were not.

Asking prices measure seller ambition. Sold prices measure market reality. When comparing homes, always start with the sold record — specifically, the sold prices of genuinely comparable properties nearby. An "online estimate" is not a sold price. An agent's valuation is not a sold price. Only the Land Registry record is a sold price.

The comparables checklist

A comparable is not just "a property that sold nearby." A genuine comparable shares these characteristics with the home you are evaluating:

1. Same property type. A flat is not comparable to a terraced house. A detached house is not comparable to a semi. If you mix property types, your comparison is worthless.

2. Similar size. A 50m² flat is not comparable to an 80m² flat, even if both are "2-bed." Floor area — in square metres or square feet — is the single most important variable in any comparison. If you do not know the floor area, you are guessing.

3. Similar condition. A recently renovated property and a probate sale in original condition are not comparable without adjustment. Condition can swing a valuation by 10-20% in the same square footage.

4. Recent sale date. A sale from five years ago tells you nothing about today's market. Twelve months is the standard window for a valuation comparable. Beyond 24 months, the sale is historical context, not evidence of current value.

5. Close geography. A property two streets away in the same postcode district is a comparable. A property in a different postcode district with different schools, transport links and buyer demographics is not — even if it looks similar on paper.

How to use price per square metre

Price per square metre is the great equaliser. It strips out the single biggest variable — size — and lets you compare properties on a like-for-like basis.

If a 60m² flat sold for £360,000 (£6,000/m²) and you are looking at a 75m² flat on the same street, the starting point for comparison is £450,000 (75 × £6,000). Adjust from there for condition, floor level, aspect and outdoor space. Do not start from the asking price — start from the £/m² floor set by actual sales.

Where to find floor area

The EPC register (epcregister.com) lists the total floor area for every property with an Energy Performance Certificate. It is free, searchable by address, and covers the vast majority of UK residential properties. If a home does not have an EPC, check the listing (Rightmove and Zoopla both show floor plans with measurements) or the Land Registry title plan.

Days on market — the invisible signal

Time on market is the clearest signal of whether a property is priced correctly. In a balanced market, a correctly priced home sells within 4-8 weeks. Faster than that suggests underpricing or exceptional demand. Slower than 12 weeks almost always means the asking price is too high.

When comparing two homes, check their listing history. If one has been on the market for 3 weeks and the other for 6 months, the difference is telling you something. Either the second home is overpriced, or there is something about it — condition, location, legal issues — that the market is rejecting. The market is never wrong about this. It just takes time to reveal the answer.

Also check how many times the price has been reduced. A property listed at £500,000 that dropped to £475,000 then £450,000 is signalling that the initial asking price was fiction. The eventual sold price will almost certainly be below £450,000. The sold comparables will confirm.

Yield — the investor's comparison tool

If you are comparing homes as investments rather than homes to live in, yield is your primary metric. Gross yield is annual rent divided by purchase price. Net yield deducts costs (service charge, ground rent, maintenance, void periods, management fees).

A useful rule of thumb: in most UK city centres, gross yields below 4% mean you are betting on capital appreciation. Above 6% means the property generates positive cash flow after a typical mortgage. Between 4-6% is the zone where the numbers need detailed modelling — tax, leverage, and holding period change the answer.

Always compare yield against the local average. A 5% yield in central London is exceptional. A 5% yield in a northern post-industrial town is below average. Context is everything.

What not to compare on

Photos. Every property looks good in professional photos. Wide-angle lenses make rooms look 30% larger. Photos tell you about staging, not about value.

EPC rating alone. Two homes can have the same EPC band but wildly different running costs. Check the actual kWh/m² figure on the certificate, not just the coloured band.

Council tax band. Council tax bands were set in 1991 (England) or 2003 (Wales) and have never been reassessed. A Band D property in one street might be worth twice as much as a Band D in another. The band tells you about 1991 values, not today's.

Online estimates. Never compare two homes using their Zoopla or Rightmove estimates. These are black-box models that may use different inputs for each property. Comparing two estimates compounds the error of both. Use sold prices.
