Why a home is not selling

By Honestly · Diagnosing stalled listings against the sold record

A home that sits on the market for months is not unlucky. It is mispriced, misrepresented, or caught in a structural friction that the seller has not diagnosed. Here are the four reasons homes do not sell — and how to tell which one applies.

1. The asking price is too high (and the market knows it)

This is the answer roughly 80% of the time. The seller or the agent set an asking price above what the sold evidence supports. Buyers view it, compare it to what nearby homes actually sold for, and walk away. The property accrues days on market. The longer it sits, the more buyers assume something is wrong — and the more the eventual discount needs to be.

How to check

Pull the sold prices of genuinely comparable properties nearby — same type, similar size, last 12 months. If the asking price is more than 5% above the sold median, the property is overpriced. If it is 10% or more above, it will not sell at that price. Period.

The agent who valued it may have quoted high to win the instruction — the highest number often lands the listing. Or the seller may have insisted on a figure the agent knew was unrealistic. Either way, the market verdict is the same: no offers, or offers well below asking.

The fix is not a small reduction. A property that has been on the market for 3 months at £500,000 will not suddenly sell at £490,000. The market has already priced in the staleness. A meaningful reduction — 5-10% — is usually required to reset buyer perception and generate fresh interest.

2. Down-valuation by the lender's surveyor

A buyer agrees a price. The mortgage lender sends a surveyor. The surveyor values the property below the agreed price. The lender will only lend against the surveyor's figure. The buyer cannot bridge the gap. The sale collapses.

Down-valuation is the single most common cause of fall-throughs in the current market. It happens because surveyors — unlike estate agents — have no incentive to inflate. They are paid by the lender to protect the lender's capital. If the sold comparables do not support the agreed price, they will say so. Their report is admissible in court. An agent's valuation is not.

Who is affected most

Buyers with small deposits (90-95% LTV) are most exposed. If a £300,000 agreed price is down-valued to £280,000 and the buyer only has a 10% deposit, they need to find an extra £18,000 in cash — the £20,000 gap minus the £2,000 deposit saving. Most cannot. The sale dies.

Sellers can pre-empt down-valuation by getting an evidence-backed valuation before listing. If you know the sold comparables and the £/m² floor, you can set an asking price that a surveyor is likely to support. 3. The property has a structural or legal defect

Sometimes the price is right and the property still does not sell. The issue is the property itself:

  • Japanese knotweed — most lenders will not lend without a management plan in place. A surprising number of sellers do not know they have it.
  • Short lease — below 80 years, marriage value kicks in and the cost to extend rises sharply. Below 70 years, many lenders will not touch it.
  • Cladding / EWS1 issues — post-Grenfell, any building over 11m with cladding needs an EWS1 form. Without one, the property is effectively unmortgageable.
  • Structural movement — subsidence, heave, or historic underpinning. Even if repaired, some buyers and lenders will walk away.
  • Title defects — missing deeds, restrictive covenants, ransom strips, or unregistered land. Solicitors spot these; buyers rarely do until it is too late.
  • Flood risk — Environment Agency flood zone 2 or 3. Insurance becomes expensive or unavailable. Some lenders refuse outright.

If a property has been on the market for 6+ months with no obvious pricing issue, order a Level 2 HomeBuyer survey (or read the existing one if it exists) and check the Land Registry title register. The answer is usually in one of those two documents.

4. Chain and timing friction

Sometimes the property is fine and the price is right, but the transaction is stuck in a chain:

  • Upward chain break: The seller's purchase falls through. They cannot complete on the sale because they have nowhere to go.
  • Downward chain break: The buyer's sale falls through. They cannot complete on the purchase because they have not released their equity.
  • Probate delays: The property is part of an estate. The Grant of Probate has not been issued. No exchange can happen until it is.
  • Leasehold delays: The freeholder or managing agent is slow to provide the management pack. This is routine and infuriating. It can add 6-8 weeks to any leasehold transaction.

Chain friction is the hardest problem to solve because it is outside your control. The only defence is information: know whether the seller is in a chain, whether they have found onward, and whether their buyer is chain-free. A chain-free buyer at a lower offer is often a better outcome than a higher offer dependent on a 3-link chain.

What to do if your home is not selling

Step 1: Get the sold evidence. Pull the last 12 months of sold prices for comparable properties within 0.5 miles. If your asking price is above the sold range, reduce it to within the range. Do not reduce by 2% and hope. The market has already told you the answer.

Step 2: Check the listing quality. Bad photos, no floor plan, missing EPC — these signal neglect and reduce viewings. Professional photos and a measured floor plan pay for themselves in a single week of reduced time on market.

Step 3: Check for surveyor traps. Order your own survey before listing, or at minimum check for the obvious: lease length, cladding status, flood zone, and any planning applications on neighbouring properties. Fix what you can. Disclose what you cannot.

Step 4: Consider the agent. If the agent overvalued to win the instruction and has done nothing to correct it after 8 weeks with no offers, they are not working for you. They are working for their pipeline. Switch agents or renegotiate the asking price with evidence.
